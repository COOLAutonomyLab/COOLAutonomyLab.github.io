# <i class="fas join"></i>Join our Lab! Please check below.

The goal of COOL Autonomy Lab is to create a supportive and fun research environment for students to learn and develop necessary skills for their future careers. We are always interested in working with strongly motivated students with a strong mathematical background. If you are passionate about designing the next generation of control, optimization, and learning algorithms for autonomy, feel free to reach out to me. Below are some guidlines and information what I expect from you and vice versa. 

## PhD Students

**1. What is my goal for PhD students?**

I always believe in a strong foundation. That said, students will have opportunities to learn fundamentals that will enable them to tackle their research problems. When they graduate from our lab, they should have not only a good foundation but also deep understanding and some results of one research area that they choose during their PhD. These intial results will hopefully enable them to  be successful wherever they will end up with.              

**2. What type of research do I focus on: Theoretical vs applied?**
 
In general, our research lies on the intersection of theory and applications. We want to develop new efficient methods with theoretical guarantees to solve practical problems. We also want to bring what we design to verify on simulations and real hardware, e.g., robots. Ideally, we want to have a balance between theory and implementation.  

**3. What do I expect from incoming PhD students?**

In general, I expect that incoming students will eventually teach me new knowledge about some research areas. Here are more specifics. First, students joining my group should feel comfortable to take theory classes in control, optimization, and probability from AE, ECE, and Math departments. While taking courses (during your first year), students should explore different research areas and should know what problems you want to solve. Once you have sufficienly finised courses for your degree (probably in your second year), you should fully focus on addressing challenging research questions in your desired areas. That said, I expect that students will be selfmotivated and indepedent in your research. Eventually, this is your degree. I already finished mine!            

**4. How often will I interact with PhD students?**

I am very hands-on person or at least I try to. I'm always interested in learning new things, and one way is through my students. I always want to meet with my students one time every week to get an update on what they have been working on and how can I help. I believe that to be succesful, students and their advisor should communicate with each other well. Here are a few examples that I don't want to have. 

1) Ghosting is extremely bad: It can mean you don't work and don't have anything to discuss or we don't like each other and can't talk. So, if we schedule a meeting, I will definitely not ghost you and expect you do the same.
2) If you disappear and show up in a few weeks later with a nice result, I don't like it. If you can work on your own then why you need me. 

**5. How many papers do I expect from PhD students per year?**

I do not have a golden number. I think students should publish one or two conference papers (from their second year) that will enable them to attend conferences to network with their peers. At the end, I prefer journals because we have to go deeply to our research to have sufficiently good results to get accepted.      

**5. How do I decice when a student can graduate?**

I do not decide when a student can graduate. There is a committee from the department for that. To some extend, my role, as your advisor, is to tell you my opinion whether you are doing well for your PhD. At the end, you will be the one to decide whether you are ready to gradute, I think. In general, you should make sure that you will not finish your PhD in 7 or more years, which is unsual in engineering.
